generalised wick's theorem

categories: physics

Dec 6, 2019

Required level: 4th year theoretical physics.

If you had an undergraduate or an introductory course in quantum field theory, you were probably introduced to Wick's theorem, which states that the time-ordered product can be transformed into a sum of normal-ordered products:

\begin{align} \label{hello} \mathcal{T}\left\{ \hat{\phi}(x_1) \dots \hat{\phi}(x_n) \right\} = \mathcal{N}\left\{ \hat{\phi}(x_1) \hat{\phi}(x_n) \dots \hat{\phi} + \text{all possible contractions} \right\}, \end{align}

where $\mathcal{T}\{\dots \}$ refers to the time-ordering and $\mathcal{N}\{\dots\}$ refers to normal-ordering.

This is one version of Wick's theorem; there is another generalised version which I think makes clear what Wick's theorem is doing.

For this version, we state is with respect to some (many-body) state $\lvert \Psi \rangle$.

For any operator $\hat{O}$, we split it into three parts:

$$ \hat{O} = \hat{O}^0 + \hat{O}^+ + \hat{O}^-,$$

(the $0$ does not indicate power) such that

$$ \hat{O}^0 = \text{cst.},$$

$$ \hat{O}^- \lvert \Psi \rangle = 0,$$

$$ \hat{O}^+ \langle \Psi \rvert = 0.$$

One way to explicitly construct such operators (and how it is conventionally done) is

$$ \hat{O}^0 = \langle \hat{O} \rangle,$$

$$ \hat{O}^+ = \left(\hat{P} - \hat{O}^0\right)(\mathbb{1} - \hat{P}), $$

$$ \hat{O}^+ = \left(\mathbb{1} - \hat{P}\right) \hat{O} \hat{P}, $$

where $\langle \dots \rangle := \langle \Psi \rvert \dots \lvert \Psi \rangle$, and the projector $\hat{P} := \lvert \Psi \rangle \langle \Psi \rvert$.
